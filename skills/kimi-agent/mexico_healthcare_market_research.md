## 2. HEALTHCARE ACCESSIBILITY GAPS

### 2.1 Public Healthcare Challenges (IMSS, INSABI)

**System Overview:**
- **IMSS**: Employment-based coverage, ~54.4% of population (2023)
- **IMSS-Bienestar**: For uninsured/vulnerable, replaced Seguro Popular
- **INSABI**: Eliminated 2019, transitioned to IMSS-Bienestar

**Key Problems:**
| Issue | Impact |
|-------|--------|
| Medicine shortages | 64% of contracts unfilled (2019-2020) |
| Long wait times | Up to 3 hours average for appointments |
| Limited specialist access | Referrals required, weeks/months wait |
| Hospital overcrowding | Family expected to provide bedside care |
| Budget cuts | 14.3% YoY decrease in 1Q25 |
| Staff shortages | 2.7 doctors/1,000 vs 3.9 OECD average |

**Coverage Crisis:**
- Uninsured rate: **29.1% in 2023** (up from 6.2% in 2015)
- 20%+ of Mexicans lack ANY health insurance
- 52.7% of out-of-pocket spending goes to medications
- Catastrophic health expenditures increased 64.5% (2018-2024)

### 2.2 Private Healthcare Costs & Accessibility

**Cost Structure (2024-2025):**
| Service | Private Cost (MXN) | Private Cost (USD) |
|---------|-------------------|-------------------|
| General doctor visit | $400-800 | $20-40 |
| Specialist consultation | $800-2,500 | $40-125 |
| Emergency room visit | $1,000-10,000 | $50-500 |
| Pharmacy clinic visit | $45-60 | $2-3 |
| Hospital stay (per day) | $6,000-24,000 | $300-1,200 |

**Medical Inflation:**
- Private consultation prices rose 6.1% (April 2025) - highest since 2004
- Anti-inflammatory drugs: +7.14%
- Diabetes treatments: +6.77%
- Projected 2025 medical inflation: **14.9%** (vs 10.1% Latin America average)

### 2.3 Doctor-to-Patient Ratios by Region

- Mexico: 2.4 doctors per 1,000 people (OECD average: 3.5)
- Nurses: 3.0 per 1,000 (OECD: 9.2)
- Hospital beds: 1.0 per 1,000 (OECD: 4.2)

## 5. COVID-19 IMPACT ON TELEMEDICINE

### 5.1 Adoption Acceleration

**Pre-COVID (2019):**
- Less than 30% of hospitals used telemedicine
- Limited regulatory framework
- Low patient awareness

**During COVID (2020-2021):**
- Telemedicine consultations increased **300%+**
- Telemedicine saved 12,339 in-person journeys and 10M pesos (2020 vs 2019)
- PEMEX implemented teleconsultation guide (pioneer)
- Video consultations became lifeline for non-COVID patients

### 5.2 Permanent Shifts in Patient Behavior

**Lasting Changes:**
- E-pharmacy adoption sustained post-pandemic
- Consumer expectation for same-day delivery
- Comfort with digital health tools increased
- 40% of consultations projected to be telemedicine by 2026

**Market Growth:**
- Latin America telemedicine: USD $1.5B (2019) → 20.5% CAGR through 2026
- Mexico telemedicine: USD $1.2B projected by 2026

### 5.3 Regulatory Changes

**Ley General de Salud:**
- Provides framework for digital health
- Lacks clear guidelines for video consultations (still ambiguous)
- LFPDPPP (data protection) compliance required

**Digital Prescriptions:**
- National digital prescription platform launched (2024)
- Improves prescription legibility
- Streamlines patient record management

**Remaining Barriers:**
- Only 30% of private providers fully integrated digital care (vs 60% Brazil)
- Regulatory uncertainty for providers
- Data security concerns

## 6. CULTURAL FACTORS

### 6.1 Mexican Attitudes Toward Technology in Healthcare

**General Sentiment:**
- Cautiously optimistic about health AI
- Physician enthusiasm tracks with familiarity
- Public wariness unless benefits clearly explained
- Trust drops when care advertised as "AI-assisted"

**Key Research Findings:**
- Information quality is strongest predictor of trust in AI
- Personalization increases adoption intention
- Autonomy in AI systems builds trust
- Transparency about data use critical

### 6.2 Trust in AI vs Human Doctors

**Trust Factors (Research-Based):**
| Factor | Impact on Trust |
|--------|-----------------|
| Information quality | Strongest positive (β=0.447) |
| Situational normality | Positive (β=0.237) |
| Personalization | Positive (β=0.151) |
| Autonomy | Positive (β=0.147) |
| Anthropomorphism | Not significant |
| Social influence | Negative (β=-0.115) |

**Implications for Service:**
- Emphasize real physician involvement (not just AI)
- Ensure high-quality, evidence-based information
- Personalize interactions
- Be transparent about AI role
- Avoid over-hyping AI - focus on outcomes

### 6.3 Preference for In-Person vs Virtual Consultations

**Current Preferences:**
- In-person still preferred for serious conditions
- Virtual accepted for: follow-ups, prescriptions, minor ailments
- Chronic disease management: Growing virtual acceptance
- Mental health: Increasing virtual comfort

**Barriers to Virtual:**
- Physical examination limitations
- Technology comfort (older generations)
- Trust in diagnosis without physical touch
- Preference for personal relationship with doctor

**Opportunity:**
- Hybrid model: AI triage → virtual consult → in-person if needed
- Follow-up care ideal for virtual
- Prescription renewals
- Chronic disease monitoring

### 6.4 Family Involvement in Healthcare Decisions

**Cultural Characteristics:**
- Family-centered decision making common
- Multiple family members may attend consultations
- Elderly care often family responsibility
- Women over 35 are primary healthcare decision-makers for families

**Service Implications:**
- Allow family member participation in consultations
- Enable proxy booking for elderly/children
- Family plan pricing attractive
- WhatsApp groups for family health management

### 6.5 Traditional Medicine Usage

**Current Practice:**
- Curanderos (traditional healers) still consulted, especially rural
- Hot springs, sweat baths used in indigenous communities
- Modern medicine sometimes viewed as expensive or untrustworthy
- Integration approach: Respect traditional practices while offering modern alternatives
